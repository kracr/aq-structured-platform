Introduction

Air pollution is a significant problem across the major cities of the World and is also a part of the sustainable development goals. Tackling it, however, is a rather complex task as the solution lies at the intersection of society, politics, science and technology. Air pollution has substantial spatial variance. Even within one city, air quality can vary a lot depending on the locality, close proximity with industrial areas, population density etc.

In order to tackle air pollution, the government agencies frame policies for mitigating the sources of pollution, increasing the civic engagement and promoting the standardized air quality indices such as the Air Quality Index (AQI). In this work, we delve into the latter two aspects, i.e., improving the community engagement and studying AQI from the perspective of different social cohorts. This lead to a socially relevant AQI, which we named, Social AQI (SAQI).

We conducted an ethnographic survey at six different locations in Delhi, India, which is one of the most polluted cities in the World. At these six locations, we have also deployed air quality sensors to record the hyperlocal AQI. The details of the data collection and the survey results are discussed in Section. In order to model and integrate these disparate and heterogenous datasets, we built a SAQI Ontology (SAQIO). This ontology captures the relationship between the seemingly unrelated concepts in the datasets, which in turn are used to construct a Knowledge Graph (KG) from these datasets. A SAQI app is built on top of the KG. This app takes into account the different social cohorts and their understanding of the AQI. The following are our contributions.

  1. An ontology (SAQIO) that models the data from the air quality sensors and the ethnographic survey data. SAQIO is built using our Pollution ontology design pattern. SAQIO is available at https://github.com/XXXX/XX-XXXXXXXX-XXXXXXX and the documentation is at https://saqi-er24.netlify.app/saqi.

  2. An SAQI KG that integrates the air pollution data from local and central sensors with the data from the ethnographic survey. YARRML is used to convert the sensor and survey data into a KG. These mappings, along with the SHACL constraints and SPARQL queries on the KG, are available at https://dx.doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.XXXXXXX. The SPARQL endpoint over the KG is available at https://https://saqi-er24.netlify.app/saqi/sparql/#/dataset/aq-store/query.

  3. An SAQI App that is built using the KG to encourage community engagement and response. This is available at https://saqi-er24.netlify.app/.

  4. The two datasets – ethnographic survey questionnaire and responses and the hyperlocal air quality data from six different locations in Delhi. This is available at https://dx.doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.XXXXXXX. These datasets will be helpful to social scientists, environmental scientists, biophysicists and biochemists.

Related Work

There are a few existing ontologies related to air pollution and air quality. Adeleke et al.,  built an ontology to model pollution as a part of a web of IoT sensors. Their ontology is used in an indoor air pollution monitoring and control application. Mihaela et al., , developed an ontology to model air pollution. They used human experts and heuristic rules generated through machine learning techniques as knowledge sources. They use the ontology to monitor and control air pollution in urban regions. Riga et al.,  use ontologies to make efficient environmental decision support systems. Ontologies are used to model the status of the environment and its impact on citizens’ health and actions. Mertal et al.,  use CityGML and aim to connect 3D city models with air pollution ontologies for sustainable urban development.

Some of the applications related to air pollution have focused on categorizing AQI value into good, poor and severe to generate recommendations on actions to be performed. Examples of such applications are CPCBcrr5 by CPCB, AQICN6 and SAFAR7. The forecasting system developed by SAFAR predicts air quality and generates recommendations a few days ahead. Urban Emissions 8 analyses the contribution of components such as traffic, dust and wind on the AQI at a particular location. They make use of a chemical transport model system known as WRF-CAMx for integrating the data. All the applications discussed here do not generate spatiotemporally aware recommendations and do not take into account the social makeup of a locality. Instead, they rely on centrally located sensors that may not be able to capture the complete dynamics of air pollution at a location.

Air quality research generally involves analyzing pollution sources, government policies, and their adverse effect on public health. For example, Agarwala M. and Chandel A. explain the role of crop residue burning on Delhi’s air quality  and Huang et al., studied the emission of pollutants from power generation plants . We focus on social factors operating at the individual and institutional levels that affect the public’s perception and awareness of pollution. We use an ontology based approach to integrate the air quality data with the ethnographic data of locations.

Requirements Analysis

We conducted a workshop on AQI and its social implications. The participants of this workshop include officials from the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB)9, social scientists, field workers and technologists. During the discussion, several issues were raised, such as the coverage of the centrally deployed sensors, the impact of air pollution on different social groups (students, daily wage workers, athletes, shopkeepers, etc.), the awareness about air pollution and AQI among the different social groups and the citizen participation to tackle the problem of air pollution. In order to get answers to these questions and to know the ground reality, two field workers were hired and asked to run a survey in two different regions of Delhi. These two regions were selected because they had variations in terms of demography and consisted of educational institutes, industries, dairy farms, sports academies and grocery stores. In each region, three different locations were selected to get a representation of the region’s demography and social groups.

After identifying the six locations, a field survey was conducted in these locations inquiring people about their awareness of the air quality indexes, their perception of pollution, and their views on government response towards pollution. The survey targeted six different social groups - teachers, students, mothers of young kids, local shopkeepers, sports academies, and social workers. At these six locations, air quality sensors were also deployed to study the hyperlocal AQI in the context of the social groups. Exposure to hyperlocal data increases local participation, which can make local institutions respond effectively to their local pollution problems. We term this as socializing and localizing the AQI in the neighborhood. The policies related to handling air pollution can be tuned by the local government bodies for each social group to address their specific needs. For example, the local school timings can be changed so that school children can avoid the time when the pollution is high.

The survey responses show that the AQI numbers and the data behind them are not interpretable to most people, even in Delhi, which has been one of the most polluted cities for consecutive years. Several survey respondents correctly identified the contributors to the air quality, which are crop burning, construction, and industries. However, except for the teachers and student social groups, others either did not have access to the AQI or were not able to draw any inferences from the AQI numbers. The local government bodies were also untrained to tackle the pollution locally and remained uninvolved.

Given this context, we proposed building a mobile and Web friendly application that is available in the local language. This application can increase the awareness of AQI by explaining the relevant facts and also recommend steps that can be taken locally to mitigate the problem of air pollution within a particular locality. In order to build such an application, the data from heterogeneous sources must be integrated and structured. So an ontology that can satisfy all the requirements discussed here will be appropriate for this application.

Competency Questions

From the workshop and the survey results, we collected the following minimal set of questions, also referred to as competency questions, that should be answered by the ontology.

  1. What is the air pollution literacy of a particular social group?

  2. What is the major perception of people towards air quality in a region?

  3. What is the average AQI value at a place over a period of time?

  4. How do people expect the government to mitigate the effect of air pollution in a region?

  5. What is the perception of people living in different regions towards air quality?

As can be observed from the competency questions, multiple data sources are required to answer them. They may have different structures and formats. These datasets include the AQI data, social data and meteorological data.

Methodology

The methodology of building the SAQI ontology revolved around continual iterative development. We developed the ontology in two phases – building a barebones ontology consisting of classes and properties that were minimally essential and, later on, iteratively extending it based on the feedback and the requirements of the SAQI application. The former has led to the development of the Pollution ontology design pattern. In the latter phase, feedback on the ontology is obtained from the social scientists and the field workers in our team. An example of the importance of their feedback is the presence of the Person class. Since most of the survey responses are connected to the people living in a locality, the feedback was that it would be an important concept in the SAQI ontology.

Since reusing existing ontologies and vocabularies is a good practice, we reused three ontologies that are widely known. We reused the foaf , WeatherOntology10 and schema.org 11 ontologies as shown in Table 1.

Ontology Description

The SAQI ontology is divided into three sub-ontologies, which we refer to as modules – Pollution module, built using Pollution ODP12, Trajectory module built from the Trajectory ODP 13 and Ethnography module for structural representation of field survey responses.

Pollution

The Pollution ontology is extended from the Pollution ODP as shown in Figure 1. The ODP has been adapted to the air pollution domain by extending some of the classes from the ODP. For example, the Pollutant class is extended to PrimaryPollutant and SecondaryPollutant. SecondaryPollutant is further extended to GaseousPollutant and ParticulateMatter. Some of the important concepts and properties from the Pollution module are as follows.

AirPollution. AirPollution class is extended from the Pollution class of the Pollution ODP. The Pollution class represents the different types of contaminants in the natural environment. AirPollution is linked to Pollutant through the hasContributor property (as shown in Axiom 1). The institutions working to mitigate the pollution sources through policies and awareness campaigns are linked by the object property takesActionAgainst. These properties and their connection with the AirPollution class is shown in Figure 2.

Pollution ⊑ ∃hasContributor.Contributor

hasContributor. This object property connects the AirPollution class to its direct and indirect contributors (pollutants).

takesActionAgainst. The institutions that are working to mitigate the effects of air pollution are connected through this object property. We considered two possible types of institutions – government and non-government institutions such as NGOs.

Contributor. Contributor can either be a contaminant particle, i.e., Pollutant or it could be a Carrier that transports a Pollutant. The Contributor class and other related classes are shown in Figure 3.

carriesPollutant. This object property is used with an IndirectContributor that transports a Pollutant from one spatial location to another, for example, wind and water flow.

DirectContributor. The DirectContributor class represents the contributors that directly impact pollution. Pollutant is a subclass of DirectContributor. Some examples of pollutants include biological pollutants (viruses, pathogens, bacteria, etc.), chemical pollutants (toxic metal, radionuclides, organophosphorus compounds, gases, etc.) and physical pollutants (sound, thermal energy, space debris, etc.) present in a particular environment.

IndirectContributor. The IndirectContributor concept represents concepts that indirectly contribute to pollution at a particular spatiotemporal point. These include environmental factors like temperature, the air or water streams flowing into or out of a particular place, the socio-economic factors such as policies and demographics. We have modelled only the Carrier concept as the subclass of IndirectContributor since other indirect contributors are specific to certain kinds of pollution.

Carrier. This concept is a subclass of the IndirectContributor concept and represents the air, water, and other kinds of streams flowing into or out of a particular place. It is linked to the Trajectory concept through the hasTrajectory property. Carriers are generally observed to affect the concentration of pollutants at a particular place and are important in modeling pollutants. To represent the pollutants that might be carried through a trajectory, the Carrier concept is linked to the Pollutant concept by the carriesPollutant property.

Pollutant. Pollutant is a central concept in the Pollution module. It is associated with the Observation class to keep a record of pollutant concentrations at any location and timestamp. Each pollutant has a prescribed standard that is defined by the government authorities. The Pollutant class and its connections are shown in Figure 4.

hasPrescribedStandard. Links a pollutant with standard limits based on concentration/amount of that pollutant in the environment. For example, WHO prescribed standard for PM2.5 is less than 5 μg/m3 mean concentration in the air annually.

hasObservation. This is used to capture the concentration of a particular pollutant at a given time and spatial location.

PrescribedStandardForPollutants. This is a stub meta-pattern  that can be used to describe the prescribed ranges for pollutants. For a particular location, pollutants have a defined range of permissible concentrations that are specified by various global authorities. A standard for a pollutant may change with time and place. Hence the PrescribedStandardForPollutants concept is linked to the Observation concept by the hasObservation property.

PollutantGenerator. Pollutant generators are events that generate pollution, such as crop burning, power plants, waste burning and dusty weather. It is related to the Perception class in the Ethnography module, indicating the perception of people on the severity of pollution concentration in a specific area. The PollutantGenerator class and its connections are shown in Figure 5.

nearby. This property links a pollution generator to a carrier trajectory.

SourceResponsibleForLocalPollution. The perception of pollution is directly linked to observable pollution generators in a locality, resulting in the perception of pollution by the people in that locality.

Trajectory

Concepts in the Trajectory module uses the concepts from the Pollution ODP (Figure 6). It models the trajectories of pollution carriers. For example, a wind current passing through a crop burning site may carry carbon based pollutants to another destination. This displacement of pollutants needs to be accounted for in order to get a holistic understanding of air pollution. The trajectory component has been adapted from the Trajectory ODP . A brief description of the Trajectory module concepts is given below.

Trajectory. The Trajectory concept represents a set of ordered spatiotemporal points.

TrajectoryPoint and TrajectorySegment. The Trajectory concept is linked to the TrajectoryPoint and TrajectorySegment through the hasPoint and hasSegment properties. The nextPoint property links trajectory points in the appropriate order within a trajectory. The segments in the trajectory are defined by a starting trajectory point {xi, yi, ti} and an ending trajectory point {xj, yj, tj} where ti, tj denote time points such that ti < tj . The TrajectorySegment concept represents this notion of a segment which is connected to two fixes through startsFrom and endsAt properties.

Ethnography

This ontology module captures the ethnographic details of a location we obtained from the field survey. This data and the ontology can help in framing policies and making decisions to mitigate air pollution at the hyperlocal level. The Ethnography module is shown in Figure 7.

Person. This is a core concept of this module since the ethnographic data relies on the observations of a person. The data we collect relates a person to their knowledge of air pollution and accounts for the social makeup through the aggregation of person-level data. This concept is connected to concepts such as AirPollutionLiteracy, Perception and Region through the hasAirPollutionLiteracy, hasPerception and livesIn properties.

AirPollutionLiteracy. This concept describes the air pollution literacy of the people in a locality. We assess this by asking questions14 to the people related to their awareness of AQI and air pollution in general. The AirPollutionLiteracy concept has the data properties hasAQILiteracy, hasAirPollutionGraphicLiteracy, hasParticulateMatterLiteracy and hasAQIColorLiteracy.

Perception. This concept captures the perception of an individual with regard to the level of air pollution in their local neighbourhood and the city. It consists of data properties such as howIsYourLocalityComparedToOtherAreas (the literals that can be linked using this property are “equally polluted”, “less polluted”, “more polluted”, “don’t know”), shouldPollutionIssuesBeRaisedInElections and localAirQualityRating. An aggregation of people’s perceptions in a particular region is expected to give the perception of that region.

SHACL Shapes

SHACL15 can be used to enforce constraints on the KG. The constraints are defined in shape graphs. KG need to satisfy the condition specified in the shape graphs to be validated. Shapes for the prominent classes, such as Person and AirPollution were created and the triples that were generated from the data using YARRRML mappings were validated using the SHACL constraints. An example of a SHACL shape of the Person class is as follows. The first few constraints indicate that the subject (Person in this case) should have one and exactly one value associated with a property (for example, SAQIOntology:livesIn).

      sh:nodeKind sh:IRI ;
    ] ;
  sh:property [
      rdf:type sh:PropertyShape ;
      sh:path SAQIOntology:hasPerception ;
      sh:class SAQIOntology:Person ;

      sh:minCount 1 ;
      sh:name "has individual perception" ;
      sh:nodeKind sh:IRI ;
    ] ;

      rdf:type sh:PropertyShape ;

SAQI Application

We built a mobile and Web friendly SAQI application (Figure [fig:saqi_layout])16 that makes use of the KG to answer questions related to air quality. To mitigate the knowledge barrier in the understanding of AQI, increase awareness related to air pollution and improve citizen engagement, the app is designed to address all the sections of the community, which includes tech ill-literate people majorly. This is achieved using features such as support for local language – Hindi/English, heavy/large fonts, minimal inputs from the user, text-to-speech narration on every page and colorful graphics for AQI readings. The app consists of knowledge on air pollution and its effects which caters to specific social and spatial cohorts and also acts as AQI monitoring app that utilizes a KG built using SAQI ontology

A SPARQL endpoint (Figure [fig:saqi_sparql]) is also embedded in the application with eight queries on the pollution sensors data and the ethnography data from surveys. The SPARQL endpoint can be accessed from the app at https://https://saqi-er24.netlify.app/saqi/app/#/sparql.

The ethnographic data, pollution data from local and central sensors are mapped to triples using RDF Mapping Language (RML) helper tool YARRML 17  that provides a high level format to define R2RML and RML rules. The generated triples are then uploaded to the server running Apache Jena Fuseki18 as the triple store. A cron job running on the server takes care of new triples that need to be uploaded to the server periodically, taking care of preprocessing and validation of data using SHACL constraints.

Sustainability and Maintenance of Resources

Since the YARRRML mappings are used, it is easy to generate KG from air quality sensor data and survey data. This is scalable, i.e., we can expand to more regions and get more data. As long as the format of the data does not change, the process of converting heterogeneous sensor and survey data to KG is sustainable. Over time, the SAQI ontology and the application may need changes. We will continue to work on SAQI platform and make changes whenever required. We also hope that the community will be interested and will take this up. Since all the resources are publicly available, it will be possible to make changes and share them with the community.

Conclusion and Future Work

We proposed Social Air Quality Index (SAQI), a new approach to increase awareness about air pollution awareness, democratize AQI knowledge at the neighbourhood level and improve community participation. We have developed an ODP for pollution 19, SAQI ontology to model the local and central air quality data, as well as the ethnographic survey data from six localities in Delhi. The sensor and survey data are integrated into a KG using YARRRML mappings and the SAQI ontology. We designed a neighbourhood pollution dashboard (SAQI app) that makes use of the KG to answer questions related to air pollution and the steps that each social cohort (students, teachers, mothers of young kids, athletes, etc.) can take to mitigate the effects of air pollution. The app is available in the local language. All these resources are publicly available under an Apache License 2.0 at https://github.com/XXXX/XX-XXXXXXXX-XXXXXXX and https://dx.doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.XXXXXXX.

Going forward, we plan to incorporate more social characteristics from the neighbourhood into the ontology and the SAQI app such as addresses and contact for local government officials and the local pollution generating sources. We also plan to introduce graphical visualization in the app to explain the distribution and variation of AQI with respect to the time of the day, the seasonal variations and its correlation to activities in the neighbourhood.
